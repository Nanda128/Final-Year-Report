\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Unmanned Vehicles in Search and Rescue Operations}\label{subsec:uav-applications-in-search-and-rescue-operations}

Papers like~\cite{MARTINEZALPISTE2021114937} show that Unmanned Vehicles have been proven to be effective in Terrestrial Search and Rescue operations, compared to random searching.
Extending these ideas to Maritime environments, combining UAVs and USVs have the potential to enhance MSAR operations by providing a faster response time, increased search coverage, and reduced risk to human rescuers.
However, maritime environments present unique challenges, requiring any practical implementation to optimize coverage, communication usage, and operator workload.

Drone swarms offer additional advantages by enabling parallel searching, in addition to redundancy in case of failure at either the drone level or in communications.
However, they also create entirely new coordination challenges, such as collision avoidance, and excessive overlap.
There's also the risk of human operators abstracting the swarm away as a single entity rather than a swarm of multiple smaller entities.
BEACON seeks to build on this line of research by adopting swarm-style coverage planning in a simulation primarily focused on human supervision rather than low-level control.

\subsection{Division of Labour}\label{subsec:division-of-labour}

Deciding on the optimal search patten for a swarm of drones is a heavily involved process, requiring several parameters to be taken into account, such as the number of drones, the size of the search area, and the expected number of targets.
The challenge of deciding optimal search patterns creates a form of Traveling Salesman Problem, a well-known NP-hard problem in computation optimization.

The approach we chose to implement in BEACON is based on Voronoi Tessellations, which offer an adaptive way to divide search area among drones, depending on their location.
This method allowed us to ensure that drones are assigned to search areas that are closest to them, minimizing travel time and maximizing coverage efficiency.

We further optimized the cell generation by ensuring that faster drones cover proportionally larger cells, to ensure that all drones finish their tasks at approximately the same time.
This design choice was made to minimize the risk of drones being idle, maximizing their operational efficiency.

Within these cells, we implemented a Boustrophedon or Lawnmower pattern, introduced by ~\cite{10.1007/978-1-4471-1273-0_32}, which is a search pattern used to draw a set of waypoints to optimally cover a space.
This was chosen for its simplicity and efficiency in covering large areas, backed up by existing research on the use of such patterns by UAVs (~\cite{bahnemann2021revisiting}).

\subsection{Human Factors in Supervising Autonomous Systems}\label{subsec:human-factors-in-supervising-autonomous-systems}

The core challenge in supervising multiple autonomous systems will always be managing human cognitive load while still preserving situational awareness.
Endsley's work on Situation Awareness in the context of flight systems, ~\cite{situation_awareness}, revealed that passively monitoring highly automated systems can increase mental workload rather than decrease it.

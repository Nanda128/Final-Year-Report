\subsection{Motivation And Context}\label{subsec:motivation-and-context}

Maritime Search and Rescue (MSAR) operations are time-critical operations where survival rates correlate directly to response time.
\cite{INDONESIA2021MSARSURVIVAL} offers an example of a study that implements the Kaplan-Meier Estimator, a type of Survival Function, to determine the correlation between survival rates and response time.

However, response times are usually hampered by the issues inherent to current traditional MSAR methods, like resource constraints, uncertainty, and adaptability, as established by ~\cite{KILIC2025106692}.
Within this context, Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) / Unmanned Surface Vessel (USV) swarms present a promising solution to enhance MSAR operations.

We have existing research on the use of UAVs in Search and Rescue operations, like in~\cite{7761074}.
It found that drone swarms could provide increased search coverage, faster response times, and reduced risk to human rescuers.

However, the researchers had to rely on a team of human operators to manage drones one-to-one.
When such solutions are scaled up, human operators can quickly reach cognitive overload, reducing response times.
Such solutions are also manpower-intensive, which can be economically infeasible for some nations.

Digital Twins, which are virtual replicas of physical systems that enable real-time monitoring, offer a way to merge state information, drone control tools, and decision support into a single interface.
This has the potential to decrease cognitive load on operators, while enabling manpower-efficient drone management, since it fosters one-to-many relationships between the operator and their drones.

This project introduces BEACON, a Digital Twin Framework for Autonomous Drone Swarm Coordination in Maritime Search and Rescue operations.
Built in React, and TypeScript, BEACON simulates a swarm of autonomous drones within a bounded mission space, presenting the drone's real-time behaviour through a mission interface.

BEACON focuses on a top-down view of the mission space, with a set of drones as chosen by the user.
The user can then choose to generate search patterns, which optimally divide the search area among individual drones, depending on their position.
These drones are then given a set of waypoints to follow, before they hover-in-place unless told to Return to Base.

The primary objective of this project is to explore how combining interface design, swarm coordination methods, and task automation can create a system that reduces operator workload and maintain situational awareness while supervising several drones.
It intends to use NASA-TLX and assorted operational metrics to act as the backbone of the evaluation framework.
A secondary objective was to study the intricacies of automatic drone navigation in a maritime environment, using existing optimization solutions.
